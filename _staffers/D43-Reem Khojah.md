bio: |
  I am an Assistant Teaching Professor in Bioengineering at UC San Diego, and my academic journey began in medical laboratory science before earning my Ph.D. in Bioengineering at UCLA. My work focuses on automating biological labs in the cloud, especially using cloud-based microscopy, real-time imaging, and AI-assisted image analysis to monitor organoids — small 3D tissue models that help us study how organs grow outside the body. This project is a strong fit for students interested in computer vision, segmentation, tracking, time-series analysis, and applying data science to living biological systems. Outside academia, I enjoy mentoring students, building new educational tools, spending time with my family, and thinking about how technology can make science more accessible.

description: |
  Growing organ-like tissues outside the body is becoming one of the most exciting frontiers in biomedical engineering, drug testing, and personalized medicine. These small 3D tissue models, called organoids, can mimic important features of real organs, but they are living systems that grow, move, change shape, and respond to their environment over time. This creates a major data science challenge: how do we continuously monitor living biological samples, process microscopy images in real time, identify meaningful patterns, and eventually help automate decisions in the lab? In this domain, students will explore the intersection of cloud-based lab automation, biological image analysis, computer vision, segmentation, object tracking, time-series analysis, and AI-assisted interpretation of organoid behavior. Students interested in this area should expect to work with messy real-world biological images, imperfect labels, changing samples, and open-ended questions where data science can directly support the future of automated biology. Useful starting references include "Organoids" in Nature Reviews Methods Primers, "Multiscale light-sheet organoid imaging framework," CellProfiler for biological image analysis, "Deep learning for cellular image analysis," and "Imaging 3D cell cultures with optical microscopy."

summer: |
  Students do not need prior wet-lab experience, but they should come ready to work at the intersection of biology, imaging, cloud computing, and data science. Over the summer, students are encouraged to review the basics of biological image analysis, including image preprocessing, segmentation, object detection, object tracking, time-series analysis, and evaluation metrics for comparing automated results to manual measurements. Students should also learn the basic concepts behind organoids, why they are useful for studying organs outside the body, and why monitoring living 3D biological samples over time creates challenging datasets.

  In the summer or Quarter 1, students will begin by working with existing organoid imaging data to reproduce a simple biological image analysis workflow, such as identifying organoids, extracting size and shape features, tracking changes over time, and interpreting the results. Students will also help set up cloud/server infrastructure using SDSC resources and will collaborate with bioengineering students in the wet lab to connect imaging hardware, automation components, and biological data streams into an early digital twin system for monitoring organoid growth.

I will meet with students weekly, provide structured guidance, and help them move toward a clear end goal: producing work that can be shared through science communication before graduation, such as a poster, presentation, demo, or short report. Students will be expected to become increasingly independent in asking questions, testing ideas, documenting progress, and communicating their results. I may involve bioengineering students or other trainees for wet-lab, imaging, or hardware context, but I will remain the main mentor and weekly point of contact.

goal: |
  By the end of Quarter 1, I expect students to complete a working first version of a student-facing digital twin for real-time organoid monitoring. This should include a public-facing website or dashboard that displays live or existing organoid imaging data, a back-end workflow for image processing and analysis, preliminary analytics that track organoid features such as size, shape, movement, or growth over time, and integration with an existing motor-control robotics platform for closed-loop feedback. Students will also define the scientific question they want to pursue in Quarter 2, run the system to image a new batch of organoids in the lab to collect data and document their workflow, and prepare a preliminary manuscript-style outline that frames their work for possible publication or science communication before graduation.

notes: |
  This capstone is connected to a broader educational research effort supported by the National Institutes of Health (NIH), which focuses on building undergraduate clinical and engineering design skills through digital twin systems. Each team is expected to use its project funds, approximately $1,000 per team, to support development, testing, cloud/server needs, robotics integration, or communication of their final work.
